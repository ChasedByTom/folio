Case Study · Fintech · 2022

Making color mean something in a retail super-app.

How I rebuilt Zadinga's visual foundation — a theme and design system for India's Kirana shop owners — by taking the brand color away from most of the UI.

ProductZadinga — Kirana shop-management app
Timeline~1.5 months · late 2022
My roleDesign Guide — theme, design system, mentoring
TeamSlim squad @ Thence (agency)
ScopeTheme → design system → 5 core screens
01 — Overview

A retail app with super-app ambitions

Zadinga is a mobile shop-management app for India's Kirana (neighborhood grocery) owners — they digitize their inventory and fulfill online and offline orders, accepting or rejecting each based on live stock.

I joined an in-flight project at Thence as Design Guide — the craft and visual-system lead. My brief: set a new theme, codify it into a design system, and mentor the juniors carrying it forward. I owned the visual direction, the system, and five foundational screens — and built the client deck that justified the direction.

02 — Problem & decision

Color everywhere, meaning nowhere

The app had color without a system — a dull grey and the brand green, reused everywhere. When every button is green, color stops signaling priority: hierarchy collapses, primary actions don't stand out, and duplicate components pile up. For a product with super-app ambitions, you can't scale an interface with no foundation beneath it.

I audited the existing UI for inconsistencies and benchmarked Swiggy and Dunzo for how mature apps use color with restraint. The insight: the problem wasn't a lack of color — it was a lack of meaning assigned to it. So I made a counterintuitive call: reserve the brand green for identity and key moments, and drive routine primary actions with a neutral dark-grey gradient. I kept the green so the rebrand still felt like Zadinga — I just stopped spending it on everything.

"If every button is green, green stops meaning anything. Reserve the brand color for identity and key moments, and use a neutral for routine actions — so hierarchy and the brand both stay strong."

— the argument I built into the client deck. It won over a client strict about protecting the green.

See the pre-redesign UI in Figma
03 — Solution & impact

A system where color carries signal

I applied the new theme to the five screens — Home, Order Summary, Profile, Plan Details, and a text-heavy internal form — chosen deliberately: together they hold 70–80% of the app's unique components, so redesigning them bootstrapped the whole system. Reserved green, dark-grey primary actions, a spacing grid to kill the clutter, and duplicate components collapsed into a single documented set.

The redesign was the primary release in that window, so the ~35% lift is credibly its own. Primary actions became unmistakable and screens read calmer — but the deliverable that outlasts the metric was the theme and design system, handed off so the team could extend it without me.

04 — Reflection

What I'd do differently

I'd close the evidence loop — baseline the rating before shipping and validate with even a lightweight round of Kirana-owner feedback, rather than leaning only on an audit and competitor benchmarks. A "35% more reviews" headline lands harder with a before-number.

The lasting lesson: good design is often subtraction, and the most senior thing on a project isn't the pixels — it's the argument. My biggest contribution wasn't the dark-grey button; it was defending it with a principle clear enough that a brand-protective client, whom I never spoke to directly, still said yes.
